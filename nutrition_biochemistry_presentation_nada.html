What is Nutritional Biochemistry?
Definition
Nutritional biochemistry is the study of the chemical processes and interactions between nutrients and biological systems, focusing on how diet influences health, metabolism, and disease.
Nutrition incorporates knowledge from:
Chemistry, physiology, agriculture, genetics, psychology, geography, economics, politics, and culture.
Nutrition studies:
• Factors affecting food availability
• Chemical components of food
• Effects on health and disease risk
• Actions of food chemicals on the body
Introduction to Biochemistry
The chemistry of living beings — the chemical basis of life
What is Biochemistry?
The Word "Biochemistry"
Means the chemistry of living beings, or the chemical basis of life. It is a branch of life science which deals with the study of biochemical reactions and processes occurring in living cells of organisms.
Medical Biochemistry
Deals with the chemical basis of the human body, including biochemical constituents, their interactions in body cells, and how they maintain normal health, growth, and reproduction.
Clinical Biochemistry
Deals with clinical diseases and pathological conditions. Supports diagnosis, therapy, and research in the medical field.

Metabolism
Definition
Metabolism is the sum total of all chemical reactions taking place in the cells of living organisms, including digestion, transportation, and energy production.
1. Catabolism
The degradative process — breakdown of complex molecules into simpler ones with a release of energy (ATP). Example: Glycolysis breaks 6-C glucose into 3-C pyruvic acid.
2. Anabolism
The biosynthetic process — formation of complex molecules from simple precursors. Requires and consumes energy. Example: Amino acids assembled into proteins.
Roles of Important Biomolecules
Carbohydrates
Primary source of energy for the body.
Lipids
Secondary source of energy.
Proteins
Structural and functional units; prime importance for survival.
Vitamins
Fat- and water-soluble; serve as accessory growth factors.
Minerals
Inorganic elements (major & minor); important for body structure and function.
Enzymes & Hormones
Biocatalysts and chemical messengers that regulate metabolic pathways.
Water — Biological Importance
• Represents about 65% of body weight in adult males (55% in females)
• The medium in which all chemical reactions occur inside cells
• The universal solvent of the body
• Regulates and maintains constant body temperature
• Principal constituent of all body fluids — blood, lymph, tissue fluid
• The body's main transporting agent
• Main constituent of secretions: saliva, gastric juice, bile, intestinal juice, sweat
• Main constituent of excretory fluid — urine
Water Balance
Water Input (2500 ml/day)
Drinking fluids: 1200 ml
Food ingestion: 1000 ml
Metabolic water: 300 ml
Regulated by thirst mechanism
Water Output (2500 ml/day)
Urine: 750–1500 ml
Perspiration + expired air: 500–800 ml
Feces: 100–150 ml
Regulated by ADH & Aldosterone
Advanced Topics
Nutrigenomics & Nutrigenetics
Cutting-edge topics exploring the interaction between diet and genes. Nutrigenomics examines how nutrients affect gene expression; nutrigenetics studies how genetic variations influence responses to specific nutrients — central to personalized nutrition.
Oxidative Stress & Antioxidants
Examines the biochemical basis of oxidative stress — an imbalance between reactive oxygen species and the body's detoxification capacity. Dietary antioxidants can mitigate cellular damage and help prevent chronic diseases like cardiovascular disease and cancer.
